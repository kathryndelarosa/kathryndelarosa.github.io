December 2, 2016
Fat Pig forms a trilogy with Neil LaBute's plays The Shape of Things and reasons to be pretty, with the conceit of exploring our obsession with physical appearance. To do so as theater, in our field where conventional beauty really does come close to currency, is a challenge that has led me to question: how does this obsession manifest in ways that aren't referring to a woman as "fat sow"? I hear it in calling cheat days "fat days," in counting calories as conversation, and in checking nutrition facts "like a Bible scholar," as Tom jokes in Fat Pig. If there's a distinction between a culture that encourages healthy living and a culture that subliminally screams that to have a fat body is a fate worse than death, it's a blurry one. In Helen's words: "The trick is getting other people to be okay with it." The fight to celebrate and to exist in a fat body is a fight against an inescapable culture that Helen and her actor Bridget face every day. LaBute's recent fall from grace, if grace is the residency at off-Broadway's MCC Theater he's held for years, has been cause for celebration by feminist theater makers - and a source of anxiety for our production, which started rehearsal two weeks after the news broke and whispers of #MeToo followed. I read Fat Pig with some mercy. While Carter and Jeannie fulfill the requisite LaBute monstrousness, I think he casts a compassionate eye to Helen that is infrequent in the rest of his writing. I hope this is one of the last productions of his work we do.
