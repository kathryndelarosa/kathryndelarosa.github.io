I've studied western astrology seriously since 2017, and I wrote and illustrated the Indiana Daily Student's weekly astrology column with a fraction of the knowledge I have now. I started doing more formal readings for friends in 2020, due to a lack of parties where I'd squint at Astrodienst on my phone and yell about people's Saturns, along with the focused study that comes with starting an immunosuppressant a month before a pandemic.

If you know me, I neither own crystals or think my tarot decks have feelings. I understand astrology as a man-made system, just like language or religion, created in the context of a certain time to serve the interests of a certain class. It’s not dissimilar to how I think of Catholicism: a tradition with a (less violent) history you have to study before you break and remake it into something new.

I use astrology to describe given circumstances, not determine outcomes. Chart interpretation is less magic or counseling than it is translation. To me, the stars neither move us or amount to anything more prescient than objects assigned particular meanings. My practice is more concerned with external conditions than interiority or divinity, with the heavens as a scale model for our personal and social dynamics, including our access to or restriction from resources.

While I'm not exclusively a Hellenistic or traditional astrologer, it's where I start, and then incorporate modern concepts as needed. I use whole sign houses, traditional rulerships, only the five classical aspects, and don't emphasize the outer planets or asteroids. This means the chart I delineate might have different house placements than what you're used to if you've seen your chart before, and won't include Uranus, Neptune or Pluto by default.
