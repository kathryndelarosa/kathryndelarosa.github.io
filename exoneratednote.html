December 2, 2016
Wrongful conviction and execution are national issues that are argued with numbers and charts and legalese, but the heart of any policy is human, as you will see in The Exonerated. The six people whose stories we have mounted onstage are a fraction of an unknown statistic. Over 150 people have been exonerated from death row as recognized by the Death Penalty Information Center. However, there is no way to know how many innocent people have been executed by our government, out of over 1,400 executions carried out since 1976. The six of The Exonerated are six out of an unknown number who had the will and the resources to fight for their lives through the legal system, because while that system imprisoned them, it also, begrudgingly, freed them. We do not know how many people have arrived at death row and forfeited their lives, forgone the lengthy and expensive appeals process that might have freed them. We do not know how many of the nearly 3,000 people currently on death row are innocent. More urgently, we cannot know. Post-conviction, the presumption is guilty until proven innocent. Post-execution, no one can defend themselves. Public support for the death penalty in America stands at its lowest in decades at 49%, according to a recent Pew Research Center poll. While the death penalty is legal in 31 of the 50 states, including Indiana, the number of murder cases that end with the sentence of death is decreasing every year. The majority of counties in the U.S. do not seek the death penalty, even for the most heinous crimes. Indiana’s death row population is on the low side, nationally, at 12. It’s comforting to see these facts and call it progress. But that progress is slow, and people on death row feel its inadequacy as we can never imagine. We see it in The Exonerated, in the stolen years, the stolen trust, and the stolen dignity these six are trying to regain. This play is their story. However, in the narrative of the death penalty in America, there are two groups whose stories aren’t told in The Exonerated: people responsible for wrongful convictions and people on death row who are truly guilty. The ensemble plays the different faces of our justice system, which is built to correct itself, ideally. But it does this slowly, and at the cost of lives. The anonymity of justice shown in this play absolves the actual prosecutors and police officers in these cases, in a manner that dehumanizes them. By flattening them to their worst parts, it becomes easier to hate them. It’s important to remember that these people are not flat. To us, these are public officials we elect. They’re our neighbors, friends and family. It’s important to remember that our justice system was built and is carried out by humans with human error, and that we as a public participate in it through elections and juries. Malice is rampant in The Exonerated, but as we try to self-govern today, so are mistakes. We must also ask ourselves an uncomfortable question: Where do the guilty stand in our view of capital punishment? Rather, on what foundation is our opposition to the death penalty built? Championing the wrongfully convicted is a noble cause, but it’s also the less divisive avenue for anti-death penalty activism. Do we value the lives of innocents more than we value the lives of the guilty? The Exonerated is a piece of political theater meant to rouse conversation that probes and discomfits. As Delbert says at the end: “It is necessary to be curious and dangerous to dwell here, to wonder why and how and when is dangerous —  but that’s how we get out of this hole.”
